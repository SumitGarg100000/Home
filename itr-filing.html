Frequently Asked Questions
Why is Form 16 for government employees calculated from March to February, and how is it handled in ITR?

Government employees receive Form 16 based on a fiscal year from March to February due to administrative practices in government accounting. Salaries and TDS are calculated for 12 months (March to February), and the March salary of the next financial year is adjusted separately.

Calculation Logic: The government’s financial year aligns with the budget cycle, and TDS is deducted based on estimated annual income from March to February. Any additional income or deductions (e.g., March salary, arrears) are adjusted in the next Form 16 or via advance tax.

ITR Handling: In ITR (e.g., ITR-1), report the total salary and TDS from Form 16 under “Income from Salary.” If March salary is paid in April, it’s included in the next FY’s Form 16. Verify with payslips to ensure accuracy.

Example: A government employee earns ₹50,000/month from March 2024 to February 2025 (₹6 lakh). TDS of ₹30,000 is deducted. In ITR for FY 2024-25, report ₹6 lakh as salary and ₹30,000 as TDS. If March 2025 salary (₹50,000) is paid in April 2025, it’s reported in FY 2025-26 ITR.

Market Practice: Tax professionals use Form 16 data, cross-check with payslips, and ensure no double-counting of March salary. Always file ITR-1 by 31st July to avoid penalties.

What deductions and exemptions are allowed in the new tax regime?

The new tax regime offers lower tax rates but limits deductions compared to the old regime. Key exemptions and deductions allowed include:
- Standard Deduction: ₹75,000 for salaried individuals (FY 2024-25).
- Section 80CCD(2): Employer’s contribution to NPS up to 10% of salary (14% for government employees), no upper limit.
- Other Exemptions: Gratuity, leave encashment (on retirement), and VRS proceeds (up to ₹5 lakh) are exempt.
Note: Interest on let-out property is not allowed as a deduction in the new regime, unlike the old regime (up to ₹2 lakh under Section 24).

Example: A salaried individual with ₹10 lakh income and ₹50,000 employer NPS contribution claims ₹75,000 standard deduction and ₹50,000 under 80CCD(2). Taxable income: ₹8.75 lakh. Tax in new regime: ~₹45,000 (after rebate).

Always compare old vs. new regime to choose the most beneficial option.

How is F&O income shown in P&L for ITR, including futures, options, and intraday?

Futures and Options (F&O) income is treated as business income under Section 43(5) (non-speculative for F&O, speculative for intraday). In the Profit & Loss (P&L) statement for ITR-3 or ITR-4, report as follows:
- Turnover: Sum of absolute profit/loss from each trade (not full sale/purchase value). For options, include premiums received on selling.
- Profit: Net of all profitable and loss-making trades.
- Expenses: Brokerage, STT, and other trading costs.

Futures Example: Buy 1 lot NIFTY futures at ₹20,000, sell at ₹20,500. Turnover: ₹500 (profit). Net profit: ₹500 - ₹50 (brokerage) = ₹450.
Options Example: Sell 1 call option, premium ₹200; buy back at ₹150. Turnover: ₹200 (sale premium) + ₹50 (profit) = ₹250. Net profit: ₹50 - ₹10 (brokerage) = ₹40.
Intraday Example: Buy 100 shares at ₹100, sell at ₹95. Turnover: ₹500 (loss). Net loss: ₹500 + ₹20 (brokerage) = ₹520 loss.
ITR Reporting: Report turnover and net profit/loss in Schedule BP of ITR-3. Intraday losses are speculative and cannot offset F&O income.
Tip: Use broker statements to calculate turnover accurately and consult a CA for audit requirements (if turnover > ₹10 crore or profit < 6% of turnover).

Which losses can be carried forward if ITR is filed after the due date?

If ITR is filed after the due date (31st July for non-audit cases), certain losses cannot be carried forward under Section 80:
- Allowed: Business losses (non-speculative), long-term capital losses, and short-term capital losses can be carried forward even in late filing (up to 8 years).
- Not Allowed: Speculative business losses (e.g., intraday trading) and losses from house property cannot be carried forward if filed late.

Example: For FY 2024-25, if you file ITR on 15th August 2025 with ₹1 lakh business loss (F&O) and ₹50,000 intraday loss, only the ₹1 lakh can be carried forward. House property loss of ₹2 lakh is also forfeited.
File by 31st July to preserve all losses for set-off in future years.

What is the rebate under Section 87A for FY 2024-25 and FY 2025-26?

FY 2024-25 (AY 2025-26):
- New Regime: Rebate of ₹25,000 for resident individuals with taxable income up to ₹7 lakh. No tax if income ≤ ₹7 lakh.
- Old Regime: Rebate of ₹12,500 for taxable income up to ₹5 lakh. No tax if income ≤ ₹5 lakh.
FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27): Assumed same as FY 2024-25 (pending Budget 2025 confirmation).
Exclusions: Rebate not available against income from long-term capital gains (Section 112/112A) or special rate incomes (e.g., lottery winnings under Section 115BB).

Example: In FY 2024-25, new regime, income ₹6.5 lakh: Tax ₹17,500, rebate ₹17,500, final tax ₹0. If LTCG ₹2 lakh included, rebate doesn’t apply to LTCG tax (12.5% on ₹75,000 = ₹9,375).
Verify post-Budget 2025 for FY 2025-26 updates.

How is audit fees treated in ITR when booked in the current year but TDS is claimed next year?

Audit fees are booked as an expense in the current financial year in the P&L statement but often paid in the next year, with TDS deducted at payment. This creates a timing difference for ITR:
- Booking: Record audit fees as a business expense in the current FY’s P&L (e.g., ITR-3, Schedule BP).
- TDS Claim: TDS is claimed in the FY when deducted, as per Form 26AS, using the accrual basis.
Example: In FY 2024-25, you book ₹50,000 audit fees (TDS ₹5,000). Payment is made in April 2025, TDS reflected in FY 2025-26 Form 26AS. In ITR for FY 2024-25, claim ₹50,000 as expense. In FY 2025-26 ITR, claim ₹5,000 TDS against income.
ITR Treatment: Ensure expense is reported in Schedule BP, and TDS is matched with Form 26AS in the next year to avoid discrepancies. Maintain invoices and payment proofs for audits.

Why is only the last advance tax installment required under Section 44AD?

Under Section 44AD (presumptive taxation), eligible businesses with turnover up to ₹2 crore can declare 8% (or 6% for digital receipts) of turnover as profit without maintaining books. Advance tax is payable in one installment by 15th March of the FY, instead of four quarterly installments, to simplify compliance.

Logic: Turnover is often finalized late in the FY, making quarterly estimates difficult. The single installment allows accurate tax calculation based on annual turnover.

Example: For FY 2024-25, turnover ₹1 crore, 100% digital. Presumptive profit: ₹6 lakh (6%). Tax (new regime): ~₹54,600. Pay this by 15th March 2025 to avoid interest under Section 234C.
Tip: File ITR-4 and ensure timely payment to avoid penalties. Consult a CA if turnover exceeds ₹2 crore, requiring audit.

Tax Rate Changes
Section 111 (Tax on Accumulated Profits)

Tax on accumulated profits (dividends distributed by companies) is levied at 15% under Section 111, applicable to domestic companies. No changes for FY 2024-25 or FY 2025-26. Effective: Ongoing.
Example: A company distributes ₹10 lakh as dividends from accumulated profits. Tax: ₹1.5 lakh (15%).

Section 111A (STCG on Equity Shares)

Short-term capital gains on equity shares or equity-oriented mutual funds (held ≤ 12 months) are taxed at 20% (increased from 15%) under Section 111A. Effective: 23rd July 2024.
Example: Sell equity shares for ₹2 lakh (bought for ₹1.5 lakh) within 6 months. STCG: ₹50,000. Tax: ₹10,000 (20%).

Section 112A (LTCG on Equity)

Long-term capital gains on equity shares and equity-oriented mutual funds (above ₹1.25 lakh) are taxed at 12.5% (increased from 10%). Effective: 23rd July 2024.
Example: LTCG of ₹2 lakh. Taxable gain: ₹75,000 (₹2 lakh - ₹1.25 lakh). Tax: ₹9,375 (12.5%).

Section 112 (LTCG on Other Assets)

Tax rate reduced to 12.5% (from 20%) for long-term capital gains on assets like immovable property, without indexation benefit. Effective: 23rd July 2024.
Example: Sell property for ₹50 lakh (bought for ₹30 lakh). Gain: ₹20 lakh. Tax: ₹2.5 lakh (12.5%).

Section 115A (Non-Resident Income)

Tax rate for dividends, royalties, and technical fees for non-residents remains 20% (plus surcharge and cess). No changes for FY 2024-25 or FY 2025-26. Effective: Ongoing.
